Kayleigh and Nick's 2020

Well 2020 was a pretty crazy year wasn't it! Although we did have a few very dull months in total lockdown, we've been very fortunate to be in New Zealand where things were pretty normal for quite a lot of the year.

We didn't get around to making our usual calendar this year, so instead here are a selection of photographs from things we've been getting up to. Sorry that there are so many... we found it hard to cut them down!

Home & Garden

Most (well, actually all) of the other photos are of various outdoor activities, but we do spend some time at home as well... we just don't take many pictures there! But here are a few of our house and garden.

High Alpine Skills Course

In February Nick did a week long mountaineering course based at Plateau Hut underneath Mt Cook. This involved practicing glacier travel, crevasse rescue, pitching on steep snow. We were super lucky to get a week of fine settled weather, almost unheard of in the New Zealand mountains, but it did mean that being on the glacier was like being in some kind of solar oven!

Mt Alexander

Whilst Nick was on his high alpine skills course, Kayleigh joined a tramping club trip to climb mount Alexander, we had, rather ambitiously, tried to climb it a few years ago with a late start and in the snow. The route initially clibs to a hut in the forest on a fairly easy track and then becomes much steeper to the ridge, before a scramble to the summit.

Mt Liverpool

In February, over the Waitangi weekend we headed down to the Aspiring National Park. We wanted to climb the highest peak in the area, Mt Aspiring / Tititea, but the weather wasn't quite good enough for that. Instead we climbed a lower peak called Mt. Liverpool (2482 m) instead. Check out the route map.

Philistine - Rolleston Traverse

Mt Rolleston (2275 m) is one of the highest mounatins in Arthur's Pass National Park (depending on where you believe the park boundaries lie), and its proximity to the road and reasonably good rock (for the area) make it a popular mountaineering destination. A popular route is to climb nearby Mt Philistine then traverse the ridge to Rolleston. The descent from Rolleston involves abseiling down the glacier, then traversing around to low peak before descending via the "Otira Slide", which in summer is a loose rock chute.

We completed the trip in around 12 hours which is a respeoctable time, and beat Nick's previous personal best on the route by nearly 24 hours!

Remarkables Ice & Mixed Climbing Festival

In August we went to the Remarkables Ice & Mixed Climbing Festival, which is a long weekend where some of New Zealands top climbers and mountaineers volunteer to run courses for less experience people to raise money to fund mountaineering expeditions. It's held in The Remarkables, a mountain range just outside Queentown.

Unfortunately New Zealand went back to Covid Alert Level 2 just before the festival which meant some of the social actitivies were curtailed, but luckily the courses were still able to run. Nick did a course where day one was Ice Climbing and day two was Mixed Climbing, and Kayleigh did a course called "Chicks with Picks", which is a similar thing but taught by and aimed at women. Unfortunately Kayleigh took a bit of a fall and stabbed her leg with her crampon... but didn't realise until a couple of hours later having walked back to the capark quite how bad it was and had to got to A & E to have it stitched up. She missed the second day of the course, but was back in action the day after which got her a lot of credit with the hard climbers!

Fiordland

After our time in Remarkables we had the rest of the week off. Since we were down South anyway we took the opporunity to go to Fiordland which is usually a very very long long drive from Christchurch. We had wanted to do some mountaineering routes but the weather wasn't really up to it. We did attempt the Phearson - McTalbot traverse, a classic Fiordland winter route, but the rock was still wet and we weren't keen on scrambling on loose rock with a thousand metre drop on each side! Our mountaineering ambitions dashed we did some easier walking and enjoyed the views and the solitude of the Alpine Club hut instead.

Sea Kayaking in the Marlborough Sounds

For Labour weekend we decided to do something a bit different, and headed North to the Marlborough Sounds to do some sea kayaking. We've had basic sea kayaks for a while but before this trip had only done short day trips in them. We started from Havelock and paddled about 20 km up Pelorous sound to a beautfiul campsite on Putanui point which is only accessable by boat. Then the next day we paddled back down the other side of the sound. It was a bit windier than expected, which made for some quite exciting moments in our small boats, but all in all a brilliant trip that encouraged us to upgrade our kayaks to something a bit more serious.

Victoria Range

A two-night trip in late spring to the Victoria Range, a seldom visited range a couple of hours North of Christchurch.

Broken River Hut

One good thing about the South Island is that although the weather can be pretty variable, there's almost somewhere close enough to Christchurch for a weekend trip where the weather's good. One weekend in December it was raining heavily in the big mounatins, but we found a patch of nice weather slightly closer to home in the Torlesse range with a visit to Broken River Hut. Our aim was to get fit for a big trip in the summer, so we carried in some firewood to the hut - and a bottle of wine.

North Island Christmas Holiday

We had planned to spend Christmas 2020 with Kayleigh's parents who were due to visit, but for obvious reasons that didn't happen. Instead we'd planned a fairly epic 10 day trip to an Ice Plateau in a wilderness area in the South Island... but the weather forecast for the South Island for our two week Christmas break was terrible, so we decided to go to the North Island instead with our friend Eric. We planned to visit the glaciers on Mt Ruapehu which is and active volcano, but just before getting on the plane we found out the volcanic alert level for Ruapehu had been raised to level 2, the most serious before an actual eruption! This meant we couldn't go within two kilometeres of the crater lake, which had increased in temperature to 40°C.

Despite not being able to visit the crater, we were still able to do the "Round the Mountain" route, a 66 km tramp around Ruapehu. We somewhat ambitiously did this in two and a half days which was a bit hard on our feet and knees! The track stays between about 1200 m and 1500 m altitude the whole way round, but despite this there is an awful lot of ups and downs crossing deep valleys scoured by "Lahar" - mudflows - from prvious eruptions. The scenary was really varied from bush on the West side to New Zealand's only true desert landscape on the East.

After completing the Round The Mountain tramp we headed up to the New Zealand Alpine Club hut on Ruapehu ski-field. Despite being an hour and a half walk from the road end this hut has electricity and a decent kitchen so was a good place to spend Christmas. Christmas Day was about the only warm and sunny day of the holiday. We took advantage of the weather to traverse Pinnacle Ridge. The guidebook described this as "one of the best alpine adventures in the North Island" and said "the rock is generally sound". To be honest the rock was pretty loose and scary, but it was a good day out anyway.
